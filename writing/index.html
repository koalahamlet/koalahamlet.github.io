I write technical content for software companies. The work I'm proudest of has a recurring shape: take something genuinely complicated, find the thread a smart non-specialist can follow, and tell it without flattening the parts that made it worth writing about. A customer story about an airline shipping a mobile app over Christmas shouldn't read like a press release, and a "what's a zero-day" piece shouldn't feel like a Wikipedia paraphrase. Both can be good in their own way if you take them seriously.

My background is in software. I still work as an independent engineer, so I can read a codebase, follow a postmortem, and usually tell when a draft is fudging a detail to make the prose flow. That matters most on the formats I get hired for: customer stories where the specifics are the whole point, engineering blog posts where a missed nuance is embarrassing, and comparison guides where readers are making real decisions based on what's on the page.

If you have a story sitting in a Slack channel that no one has time to write up, or a product whose technical depth keeps getting smoothed out in review, get in touch.
